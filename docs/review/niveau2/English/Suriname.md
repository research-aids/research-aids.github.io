## Abstract

Dutch museum collections contain ethnographic objects originating from Suriname. Many of these objects were acquired in a colonial context. This search guide provides information on the most common ways in which these objects ended up in collections in the Netherlands and other countries in the Global North.

### Description

On the northern coast of South America, several rivers flow into the Atlantic Ocean in an area known as the Guianas—an indigenous word that likely means 'land of many waters'. One of these rivers is the Suriname River. At the time of the voyages of the Spanish conquistador Alonso de Ojeda, this area was inhabited by the Arawaks, Caribs, and Warau. From the late sixteenth century onward, seafarers from England, France, and the Netherlands visited the area in search of the mythical land of gold, El Dorado, which, according to Sir Walter Raleigh, was to be found in the Guianas. In 1651, Sir Francis Willoughby, the English governor of Barbados, founded a plantation colony along the Suriname River. This colony was conquered by a Zeeland fleet in 1667 during the Second Anglo-Dutch War.

From 1683 onward, Suriname was administered by the Society of Suriname, a private enterprise which had the West India Company, the Van Aerssen van Sommelsdijck family, and the city of Amsterdam as shareholders in equal proportions. In 1795, the assets of the Society of Suriname were transferred to the Dutch state, which governed Suriname as a colony—apart from two periods of British occupation—until 1954. In that year, Suriname, together with the Netherlands and the Netherlands Antilles, accepted the Charter for the Kingdom of the Netherlands, which was intended to mark the completion of decolonization. In 1975, Suriname gained independence.

The European private owners put enslaved indigenous people and Africans to work on their plantations. Some enslaved people managed to escape the plantations and establish free communities in the interior of Suriname. In 1760, the colonial government signed a peace treaty with one of these Maroon groups, namely the Okanisi (also known as Aukans or Ndyuka). Peace treaties with two other groups, the Saamaka and Matawai, followed in 1762 and 1769, while the Aluku (or Boni), the Pamaka, and the Kwinti never signed a peace treaty.

From the eighteenth century onward, missionaries from the Moravian Church were active in Suriname, particularly among the Saamaka. In 1865, Pope Pius IX designated Suriname as a mission territory for Dutch Redemptorists, who were assisted in their work by the Franciscan Sisters of Roosendaal and the Brothers of Tilburg. Indigenous people were converted to Christianity from the former leper colony of Batavia on the Coppename River, while the Tamarin mission station on the Cottica River became the center of the Catholic mission to the Okanisi.

### Collections of Surinamese artifacts in Europe and the United States
Ethnographic objects were brought to Europe from the very beginning of colonization. A report published in 1796 on expeditions against the Maroons, in which the Scottish-Dutch officer [John Gabriel Stedman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gabriel_Stedman) took part between 1773 and 1777, also includes descriptions and illustrations of ethnographic objects. Stedman is known to have made a donation to the stadtholder's collection of William V in 1777 and to have donated eighteen Surinamese “curiosities” to James Parkinson in 1796 for inclusion in his [Leverian collection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leverian_collection) in London, which was auctioned in 1806. The archives of the Royal Cabinet of Curiosities contain an inventory description taken directly from Stedman's report. Based on this, anthropologists Richard and Sally Price suspect that some of the objects collected by Stedman eventually ended up in the Cabinet. However, it often remains difficult to establish a direct link between Stedman's descriptions and the objects in this collection.

Another important collection from Suriname was donated to the Cabinet in 1824 by [Frederik Andreas Kühn](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q132146744), head of the military medical service in Suriname. In the accompanying letter, Kühn wrote that some of the objects came from his late brother, who had participated in an expedition to a Maroon community in 1818. As a result, it is not always easy to distinguish Kühn's donation from objects that may have originated from Stedman. Between 1825 and 1835, the Cabinet also received donations from Suriname from, among others, [Isaac Bromet](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q137830410), [Dietrich Kanngiesser](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q94852739), [Hendrik Haagen Dieperink](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q122884501), and [Adriaan François Lammens](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adriaan_Fran%C3%A7ois_Lammens). Lammens was the son-in-law and admirer of [Gerrit Schouten](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerrit_Schouten) and was responsible for donating many of Schouten's dioramas to the Cabinet.

Missionaries also brought ethnographic objects from Suriname to Europe. One of the earliest collections was donated in 1780 by missionary [Christlieb Quandt](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q94933152) to the cabinet of curiosities at the seminary of the Moravian Church in Barby. Around 1857, missionary [Johann Jansa](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q119700090) transferred a collection of objects originating from the vicinity of the Berg en Dal mission station to the archivist of the Evangelical Moravian Church in Herrnhut. Many of the objects collected by Quandt and Jansa later ended up in the [Herrnhut Ethnographic Museum](https://voelkerkunde-herrnhut.skd.museum), founded in 1878, although the sale of ethnographic items also served as a source of income for the Moravian Church. For example, part of Jansa's collection was sold in 1861 to the ethnographic cabinet of the Royal Prussian Art Chamber, which merged into the _Ethnologisches Museum_ in Berlin in 1873. In Zeist, a small collection of objects from the Zeist Missionary Society, affiliated with the Moravian Church, is on display at [The Herrnhuter House](https://hernhutterhuis.nl/). A collection belonging to the Redemptorist Fathers is housed at [Museon-Omniversum](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/research-aids/https%3A%2F%2Fn2t%252Enet%2Fark%3A%2F27023%2Fa124abe580b6a77b82bb3edf0ca7113a).

The Brethren of Tilburg created an extensive [Caribbean Heritage photo collection](https://stadsmuseumtilburg.nl/collecties?query=*:*&qf%5b%5d=tib_collectionPart_facet:Photos%20Caribbean%20Heritage), which is now digitally accessible via the Tilburg City Museum. The Utrecht Archives manages the collection of [films and audio tapes](https://hetutrechtsarchief.nl/collectie/609C5BA2890A4642E0534701000A17FD) and [prints, drawings, maps, and photographs](https://hetutrechtsarchief.nl/collectie/609C5BA737CC4642E0534701000A17FD) of the Zeist Missionary Society.

Other nineteenth-century collections include, among others, the collections [RV-399](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=rv-399) and [RV-1054](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects? query=rv-1054), assembled by Governor [Cornelis Ascanius van Sypesteyn](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornelis_Ascanius_van_Sypesteyn_ (1823–1892)) and finance administrator [Assueer Jacob Schimmelpenninck van der Oye](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assueer_Jacob_Schimmelpenninck_van_der_Oye_(1835–1915)). These were donated in 1883 and 1895, respectively, to the National Ethnographic Museum, now the [Wereldmuseum Leiden](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/research-aids/https%3A%2F%2Fn2t%252Enet%2Fark%3A%2F27023%2F77c1a0cf982b33b9e88073c4a704049b). Also the [Artis Ethnographic Museum](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/research-aids/https%3A%2F%2Fn2t%252Enet%2Fark%3A%2F27023%2F44a7a61d62ca8589d6a93e6fde593593) possessed an extensive Suriname collection, including a rare _kwakwabangi_. Curator Cornelis Marinus Pleyte published an [article](https://www.digi-hub.de/viewer/image/DE-11-001785756/394/LOG_0381/) on this subject in 1896 in the German journal _Globus_. The Artis collection was transferred in 1921 to the museum of the Colonial Institute, which opened in 1926 and is now the Wereldmuseum Amsterdam, where it can be identified by the serial letters [TM-A](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=tm-a&locations=Suriname).

#### Explorations along the rivers
International interest in Suriname grew from the late 1870s onward due to gold discoveries in the Marowijne River basin. Accompanied by the Aluku guide and boatman [Apatou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apatou_(captain)), the French physician Jules Crevaux traveled in 1877 via the upper reaches of the Marowijne to Belém in Brazil. Crevaux [donated](https://collections.quaibranly.fr/?state=%7B%22matchAllCriteria%22%3Atrue%2C%22customSearchCriteria%22%3A%5B%7B%22field%22%3A%22%2FRecord%2FConX%2FAlphaSortConX%2C%2FRecord%2FConXother%2FAlphaSort%22%2C%22fieldLabel%22%3A%22%22%2C%22searchValues%22%3A%5B%22%5BCrevaux%2C%20Jules%5D%22%5D%2C%22description%22%3A%22Personne%28s%29%20%3A%20Crevaux%2C%20Jules%22%7D%5D%7D) the objects he had collected during his travels to the _Musée du Trocadéro_ in Paris, one of the precursors to the current _Musée du Quai Branly – Jacques Chirac_. The same applied to the [objects](https://collections.quaibranly.fr/?state=%7B%22matchAllCriteria%22%3Atrue%2C%22customSearchCriteria%22%3A%5B%7B%22field%22%3A%22%2FRecord%2FConX%2FAlphaSortConX%2C%2FRecord%2FConXother%2FAlphaSort%22%2C%22fieldLabel%22%3A%22%22%2C%22searchValues%22%3A%5B%22%5BFournereau%2C%20Lucien%5D%22%5D%2C%22description%22%3A%22Person(s)%20%3A%20Fournereau,%20Lucien%22%7D%5D%7D) that Lucien Fournereau collected along the Marowijne River in 1882. Objects from Suriname that were exhibited at the 1878 World’s Fair in Paris and the [1883 World’s Fair](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/nl/research-aids/https%3A%2F%2Fn2t%252Enet%2Fark%3A%2F27023%2F6ad17cc1c619926c9a449872d3c41b0f) in Amsterdam were distributed among various ethnographic museums after the exhibitions ended; in the Netherlands, this resulted in, among other things, the series [RV-300](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/nl/objects?query=rv-300) and [RV-370](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/nl/objects?query=rv-370).

Between May 1885 and July 1886, [Herman ten Kate](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_ten_Kate_(antropoloog)) stayed in Suriname to compile the [RV-581](https://app.colonialcollections. nl/nl/objects?query=rv-581). During the same period, Leiden professor of geology Karl Martin collected specimens from indigenous peoples and the Saamaka; this collection would be donated by his widow, Hillegonda Martin-Icke, in 1949 and incorporated into the museum as series [RV-2777](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/nl/objects?query=rv-2777). In 1887, Apatou once again guided a Frenchman, namely geographer and colonial official Henri Coudreau, along the upper reaches of the Marowijne. His collection was also [housed](https://collections.quaibranly.fr/?state=%7B%22matchAllCriteria%22%3Atrue%2C%22customSearchCriteria%22%3A%5B%7B%22field%22%3A% 22%2FRecord%2FConX%2FAlphaSortConX%2C%2FRecord%2FConXother%2FAlphaSort%22%2C%22fieldLabel%22%3A%22%22%2C%22searchValues%22%3A% 5B%22%5BCoudreau%2C%20Henri%5D%22%5D%2C%22description%22%3A%22Person(s)%20%3A%20Coudreau%2C%20Henri%22%7D%5D%7D) at the _Musée du Trocadéro_. In 1888, Army Surgeon [John H. Spitzly](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q137806783) donated a collection of ethnographic objects from Suriname to the _Berliner Gesellschaft für Anthropologie, Ethnologie und Urgeschichte_.

The 1890 research expeditions by the German [Wilhelm Joest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Joest) and the Swedish [Axel Klinckowström](https:// en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axel_Klinckowstr%C3%B6m) in 1890 resulted in donations to the _Ethnologisches Museum_ in Berlin and the _Etnografiska museet_ in Stockholm, respectively. Joest’s private collection, which also included many objects from Suriname, formed the basis of the _Rautenstrauch-Joest-Museum_ in Cologne after his death. 

For the ethnographic collections in the Netherlands, the exhibition on the Dutch West Indies was particularly significant. For this exhibition, held in 1899 at the Colonial Museum in Haarlem, colonial official [Louis Constant van Panhuys](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Constant_van_Panhuys) collected objects—primarily from Okanisi—during his posting in Albina between 1893 and 1896. Today, this collection is part of the [TM-H](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/nl/objects?query=tm-h&locations=Suriname) series at the Wereldmuseum Amsterdam.

Director [Maurice Guffroy](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q122165409) of the _Compagnie des mines d'or de la Guyane hollandaise_ [donated](https://collections.quaibranly.fr/?state=%7B%22matchAllCriteria%22%3Atrue%2C%22customSearchCriteria%22%3A%5B%7B%22field%22%3A%22%2FRecord%2FConX%2FAlphaSortConX%2C%2FRecord%2FConXother%2FAlphaSort%22%2C%22fieldLabel%22%3A%22%22%2C%22searchValues%22%3A%5B%22%5BGuffroy%2C%20Maurice%5D%22%5D%2C%22description%22%3A%22Personne%28s%29%20%3A%20Guffroy%2C%20Maurice%22%7D%5D%7D) donated an extensive collection of Wayana and Aluku artifacts to the _Musée du Trocadéro_ in 1901. Three of the eight expeditions to Suriname organized shortly after the turn of the century under the auspices of the Royal Dutch Geographical Society yielded extensive ethnographic collections, which are now part of the collection of the [Wereldmuseum Amsterdam](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/research-aids/https%3A%2F%2Fn2t%252Enet%2Fark%3A%2F27023%2Fba9397040f2cf7f618e2180fb6c90208). The 1903 expedition to the Gonini yielded series [TM-401](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=tm-401) with 86 objects, the 1904 expedition to the Tapanahony yielded series [TM-402](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=tm-402) with 493 objects, and the 1904 expedition to the Toemoek-Hoemak Mountains yielded series [TM-403](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=tm-403) with 186 objects. A [jewelry box](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects/ https%3A%2F%2Fn2t%252Enet%2Fark%3A%2F27023%2Ff6308f2e103e26992fb01fbe5c4c719a) made in connection with the Gonini expedition is part of the collection of the Rijksmuseum Amsterdam. In 1904, expedition member [Claudius de Goeje](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudius_de_Goeje) donated a personal collection to the museum in Leiden, where it was cataloged under serial numbers [RV-1443](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=rv-1443) and [RV-1454](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=rv-1454).

The collections [RV-1817](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=rv-1817) and [RV-2349](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects? query=rv-2349) were donated to the museum in Leiden in 1912 and 1937, respectively. Finally, the 1926 KNAG expedition to the Wilhelmina Mountains led by Gerold Stahel yielded the series [TM-416](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=tm-416) to the museum in Amsterdam.

In late 1923, the Museum for Education in The Hague, now the [Museon-Omniversum](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/research-aids/https%3A%2F%2Fn2t%252Enet%2Fark%3A%2F27023%2Fa124abe580b6a77b82bb3edf0ca7113a), an exhibition on Suriname. The objects that [Herman van Cappelle](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_van_Cappelle_(geoloog)), the museum's first director, had collected during his stay in Suriname formed the basis for this exhibition. Among these was a [second _kwakwabangi_](https://hdl.handle.net/21.12123/43544), which is still part of the Museon-Omniversum collection. The [Tropisch Landbouwmuseum](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/research-aids/https%3A%2F%2Fn2t%252Enet%2Fark%3A%2F27023%2F4006391907ec663bd03590d049851f84), which opened in 1915, also played a role, which opened in 1915, also had a modest Suriname collection.
  
#### Scientific research during the interwar period 
During the interwar period, scientific interest in the societies of the Maroons and indigenous peoples gradually grew. The American anthropologist couple Melville J. and Frances Herskovits visited the Saamaka on the upper reaches of the Suriname River in 1928 and 1929. Approximately two hundred of the objects they collected were donated in 1929 to the _Museum für Völkerkunde Hamburg_, now the _Museum am Rothenbaum – Kulturen und Künste der Welt_ (MARKK), where the collection was assigned [serial number 30.51](https://markk-hamburg.de/files/media/ 2020/06/MARKK-AM-ab-1921.pdf). The private collection they assembled during their visits is now housed at the [Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture](https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/collections/71626000-12bb-013c-1a26-0242ac110002) in New York. The photographs are preserved in the [Eliot Elisofon Photographic Archives, National Museum of African Art](https://sova.si.edu/record/eepa.1986-029).

In 1927, 1928, and 1930, American physician Morton Kahn [collected](https://data.library.amnh.org/archives-authorities/id/amnhc_2000250) artifacts in Suriname for the [American Museum of Natural History](https://anthro.amnh.org/collections). In 1931, French industrialists Marcel Monteux and Marc Richard financed a multidisciplinary research mission to French Guiana, which produced a [collection](https://collections.quaibranly.fr/?state=%7B%22matchAllCriteria%22%3Atrue%2C%22customSearchCriteria%22%3A%5B%7B%22field%22%3A%22%2FRecord%2FEvent%2FEvent%22%2C%22fieldLabel%22%3A%22%22%2C%22searchValues%22%3A%5B%22Mission%20Monteux-Richard%20in%20French%20Guiana%201931%22%5D%2C%22description%22%3A%22Historical%20Event%20%3A%20Monteux-Richard%20Mission%20in%20French%20Guiana%201931%22%7D%5D%7D) yielded artifacts that were donated to the _Musée du Trocadéro_. In 1934 [collected](https://collections.quaibranly.fr/?state=%7B%22matchAllCriteria%22%3Atrue%2C%22customSearchCriteria%22%3A%5B%7B%22field%22%3A%22%2FRecord%2FConX%2FAlphaSortConX%2C%2FRecord%2FConXother%2FAlphaSort%22%2C%22fieldLabel%22%3A%22%22%2C%22searchValues%22%3A%5B%22%5BDamas%2C%20L%C3%A9on-Gontran%5D%22%5D%2C%22description%22%3A%22Person(s)%20%3A%20Damas%2C%20L%C3%A9on-Gontran% 22%7D%5D%7D) Leon-Gontran Damas, a student at the Institute of Ethnology in Paris who, a year later, would lay the groundwork for the _Négritude_ movement alongside Aimé Césaire and Léopold Senghor, an extensive collection of objects from along the Marowijne River for the _Musée du Trocadéro_. In 1937, Claudius de Goeje was added as an unpaid ethnographer and linguist to the third expedition to the southern border of Suriname led by Conrad Carel Käyser. The collection he assembled during this expedition at the expense of the Dutch government was donated to the Rijks Etnografisch Museum in 1938 and 1939 and is still housed at the Wereldmuseum Leiden under serial numbers [RV-2352](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=rv-2352), [RV-2363](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=rv-2363) and [RV-2404](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=rv-2404). The French ethnologist Paul Sangnier [collected](https://collections.quaibranly.fr/?state=%7B%22matchAllCriteria%22%3Atrue%2C%22customSearchCriteria%22%3A%5B%7B%22field%22%3A%22%2FRecord%2FConX%2FAlphaSortConX%2C%2FRecord%2FConXother%2FAlphaSort%22%2C%22fieldLabel%22%3A%22%22%2C%22searchValues%22%3A%5B%22%5BSangnier%2C%20Paul%5D%22%5D%2C%22description%22%3A%22Person(s)%20%3A%20Sangnier%2C%20Paul%22%7D%5D%7D) also in 1938 among the Wayana and Aluku.

Of great importance to the collections in the Netherlands is the 1940 bequest from landscape architect [Apollonius Johannes Reynvaan](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q138794292), who assembled an extensive collection of 850 objects from Suriname in the Netherlands. Reynvaan’s collection is housed at the Wereldmuseum in Leiden under accession number [RV-2452](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=rv-2452).

The 1946 expedition by the French geographer Jean-Marcel Hurault to the Marowijne River basin marks, in a sense, a turning point in the history of collecting in Suriname and French Guiana. Although the distinction is not always clear-cut, the collections gathered from that point onward—not coincidentally the year French Guiana became an overseas department of France—less explicitly bear the character of the result of a colonial territorial survey. The objects Hurault collected along the Marowijne River were [housed](https://collections.quaibranly.fr/?state=%7B%22matchAllCriteria%22%3Atrue%2C%22customSearchCriteria%22%3A%5B%7B%22field%22%3A%22%2FRecord%2FConX%2FAlphaSortConX%2C%2FRecord%2FConXother%2FAlphaSort%22%2C%22fieldLabel%22%3A%22%22%2C%22searchValues%22%3A%5B%22%5BHurault%2C%20Jean-Marcel%5D%22%5D%2C%22description%22%3A%22Person(s)%20%3A%20Hurault%2C%20Jean-Marcel%22%7D%5D%7D) at the _Musée du Quai Branly – Jacques Chirac_.

For the collections in the Netherlands, the objects collected by [C. D. H. Eijgenberger](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q138844151) from the late 1940s onward and the gifts presented to Queen Juliana during her 1955 visit to autonomous Suriname fall under this borderline category. Eijgenberger's collection, which includes rare slave shackles, was acquired by the Wereldmuseum Amsterdam in 1978 and was assigned serial number [TM-4440](https://app.colonialcollections.nl/en/objects?query=tm-4440). The gifts to Juliana were housed at the Museon-Omniversum in 1957. 

Due to the lack of an explicit colonial context, other collections established after 1954 fall outside the scope of this research aid.

### Provenance research
To research donations to various museums, one should first follow the recommendations provided in those museums’ search tools. When investigating the distribution of cultural artifacts from specific groups to museums in the Global North, it is wise not to limit one’s focus to the Netherlands. Networks in countries of origin can be particularly useful in this regard. For example, a group of Wayana people in French Guiana has set up the internet portal [watau.fr](https://watau.fr/) with the aim of mapping the Wayana heritage scattered across the globe.
 Museums in countries of origin also possess significant expertise in this area. The _Museu Paraense Emilio Goeldi_ in Belém (Brazil), the _Musée des cultures Guyanaises_ in Cayenne (French Guiana), and the Surinamese Museum Foundation in Paramaribo have joined forces to form the [Amazon Museum Network](http://amazonian-museum-network.org/en). For research on cultural heritage of the Saamaka and Okanisi, please contact the [Saamaka Museum](https://saamakamuseum.com/) and the [Diitabiki Museum Fositen Gudu](https://fositengudu.org/), respectively.

#### Wereldmuseum Rotterdam

#### Pitt Rivers Museum, Oxford
"There are 336 objects from Surinam in the Pitt Rivers Museum of which some 77 are of Maroon rather than Amerindian origin. The Amerindian material is dominated by two collections [: Butt Colson (1963) and Peter Rivière]. [...] The non-Amerindian material is almost entirely Maroon, from the Aukan, also known as Ndyuka, living on the Maroni and Lawa Rivers. Butt Colson collected 26 objects from the Aukan, one of which is listed as coming from French Guiana. To this Aukan collection is to be added a further 39 objects donated by the Cambridge Expedition to Langa Tabiki in 1965. The remaining four objects consist of three Maroon items (a musical instrument and two calabash spoons) donated by Mrs Beatrice Braithwaite Batty in 1917. She was the author of An English girl’s account of a Moravian Settlement in the Black Forest (1858), Forty two years among the Indians and Eskimo (1893), and numerous other works. Finally there is a pottery vessel in the form of a cockerel donated by Edward Archibald Parry, Bishop of Guiana, in 1908."
